# Ideation & Validation Playbook

## The Problem-First Approach

### Why Your Own Problems Are the Best Source of Ideas
When you solve your own problem, you are the world's greatest expert on that problem. You understand the nuances, the workarounds, and the pain points better than anyone else.

**Key insight:** Your problems are often niche enough that there's a market, but not so broad that a giant company has already solved it. This is the sweet spot for bootstrapped businesses.

**Examples from the playbook:**
- Pieter Levels (Nomad List) solved his own problem of finding good places to work remotely while traveling.
- A founder with Amazon selling experience built Launch Fast because they understood new seller problems from A to Z.
- Cody Schneider built a $145K marketing machine by automating his own repetitive marketing tasks.

### Becoming a More Original Person
If you don't have original problems, become a more original person. Travel, take up unusual hobbies, work in niche industries. The more unique your experiences, the more unique your problems—and therefore your business opportunities.

## The Validation Stack

### Layer 1: The Waitlist Strategy
Before writing code, prove demand exists.

**Steps:**
1. Build a visual prototype of the "magic interaction" using AI tools (Vibe Code, Cursor, Claude Code).
2. Record a screen demo of the prototype.
3. Post the demo on social media with a link to a waitlist.
4. If 100+ people sign up, you have validated demand.

**Tools:**
- Vibe Code: Mobile app prototypes.
- Cursor / Claude Code: Web app prototypes.
- Carrd / Gumroad: Waitlist landing pages.

**Caveats:**
- Works best if you already post on social media.
- If you have no audience, algorithms can still pick you up—but be patient.
- Don't be discouraged by low initial numbers. One viral post changes everything.

### Layer 2: Manual-First Approach
Get paid to learn the workflow before automating it.

**The 7-Step Framework (Dan Martell):**
1. **Identify the problem** through customer conversations.
2. **Solve manually** for 3-5 customers. Do the work by hand.
3. **Document the workflow.** What steps are required? What takes the most time?
4. **Build a clickable prototype.** Not a full product—a mockup that shows the experience.
5. **Get pre-sales.** Sell the solution before building the automated tool.
6. **Use pre-sales to fund development.** Customer money pays for the build.
7. **Automate the workflow.** Now you know exactly what to build because you've done it manually.

**Why this works:**
- Shopify started by manually setting up stores for merchants.
- FreshBooks started by manually invoicing for freelancers.
- Basecamp started as a manual consulting service.

### Layer 3: Pre-Selling Before Building
The riskiest assumption is not "can I build it?" but "will anyone pay for it?"

**Pre-Sell Models:**
- **Off-plan sales:** Sell the vision before the product exists (like Dubai real estate).
- **Crowdfunding:** Kickstarter, Indiegogo, or custom pre-order campaigns.
- **Consulting-to-product:** Get clients to pay you to build the tool for them.
- **Equity-for-distribution:** Trade equity for access to someone else's audience.

**Real example:**
A founder built a Chrome extension in 40 hours, demoed it to a creator with an audience, and traded 50% equity for instant distribution. Result: $20K MRR in 90 days. "50% of $20K is better than 50% of zero."

## The Equity-for-Distribution Playbook

### When to Trade Equity
- You have a product but no audience.
- You have a product but no marketing expertise.
- You want to de-risk distribution from day one.

### How to Structure It
1. Build the prototype (even if rough).
2. Identify creators/influencers in your niche.
3. Pitch them on becoming a co-founder (not just a promoter).
4. Offer equity based on their contribution: audience size, content quality, commitment level.
5. Give them creative freedom to market the product their way.

**Why co-founders beat promoters:**
- Promoters are paid for a one-time post. Co-founders are invested in long-term success.
- A creator co-founder with 10K MRR guarantee derisked the entire launch.
- With initial traction, you can attract more creators, run ads, and expand.

## Common Validation Mistakes

1. **Building in isolation.** No feedback loop until launch.
2. **Surveying instead of selling.** Surveys measure interest; sales measure commitment.
3. **Overbuilding the MVP.** A $600K build that never launched vs. a $0 prototype that validated demand.
4. **Ignoring domain knowledge.** Building AI video generators without understanding video production.
5. **Fear of charging.** Free users give polite feedback. Paying users give honest feedback.

## The Validation Decision Tree

```
Have an idea?
│
├─► Can you solve it manually for 3 customers?
│   ├─► Yes → Do it. Get paid. Learn the workflow.
│   └─► No → Build a prototype. Post it. Build a waitlist.
│
├─► Do people sign up / pay?
│   ├─► Yes → Build the automated product.
│   └─► No → Pivot or find a new idea.
│
└─► Launch with the creators/communities where your users already are.
```

## AI Tools for Validation

- **Claude Code / Cursor:** Build prototypes in hours.
- **Vibe Code:** Mobile app demos without coding.
- **Make.com:** Connect waitlist forms to email sequences.
- **Lovable:** Generate landing pages from prompts.
- **HTML-to-Canvas:** Create downloadable mockups and creatives.

## Validation Checklist

- [ ] Idea comes from personal problem or domain expertise.
- [ ] Can explain the problem in one sentence.
- [ ] Built a prototype (even if rough).
- [ ] Posted prototype on social media or relevant communities.
- [ ] Created a waitlist and collected emails.
- [ ] Manually solved the problem for at least 3 people.
- [ ] Got at least one pre-sale or pre-order.
- [ ] Identified the channel where users hang out.
- [ ] Ready to build only after validation is proven.
