# Pricing & Revenue Models

## The Pricing Mindset

### Charge From Day One
The most common mistake founders make is being too scared to charge money for their apps.

**Why you must charge early:**
- If you never charge, it's a side project—not a business.
- You'll have to maintain it forever. That costs time and money.
- Free users give polite feedback. Paying users give honest feedback.
- Early revenue validates the business model.
- Revenue attracts better partners, creators, and opportunities.

**The psychological barrier:**
Many makers are "incredibly scared to charge any money." They launch, get users who love the product, and then are afraid to scare them away by charging.

**The reality:**
- The users who love your product the most will happily pay.
- The users who leave when you charge weren't your real customers anyway.
- Charging filters for serious users who provide better feedback.

## Revenue Models for Bootstrapped Startups

### 1. Subscription-Based (SaaS)
The holy grail of recurring revenue.

**How it works:**
- Customers pay monthly or yearly for ongoing access.
- You maintain, update, and support the product.
- Revenue compounds: $75/month × 1,000 customers = $75K/month.

**Advantages:** Predictable revenue, higher LTV, easier to forecast.
**Disadvantages:** Some customers hate subscriptions, churn is a concern.
**When to use:** Products with ongoing value, daily/weekly tools, B2B.

### 2. Lifetime Deal (LTD)
One-time payment for permanent access.

**How it works:**
- Customers pay once (e.g., $200) and get lifetime access.
- No recurring revenue, but immediate cash flow.

**Advantages:** Customers love it, immediate cash flow, lower barrier.
**Disadvantages:** No recurring revenue, support burden grows.
**When to use:** Early-stage products, low ongoing costs, audiences that hate subscriptions.

**Real example:**
One founder launched with subscriptions. "The day that I launched a subscription product, I had several emails from members who wanted to buy the pro product asking for a lifetime membership where they were willing to pay for two plus years worth of the pro membership all upfront. That is now about 97% of the revenue."

### 3. Freemium
Free tier + paid premium features.

**Advantages:** Low barrier, word-of-mouth, data from free users.
**Disadvantages:** Free users cost money, low conversion (2-5%).
**When to use:** Network effects, strong competition, near-zero marginal cost.

### 4. Usage-Based Pricing
Pay for what you use.

**Advantages:** Fair to customers, scales with growth, reduces churn risk.
**Disadvantages:** Unpredictable revenue, customers fear runaway costs.
**When to use:** API products, infrastructure, AI tools, variable usage.

### 5. Patronage / Donations
Ask your community to support your work.

**Advantages:** No pressure, strong community, accessible to everyone.
**Disadvantages:** Unpredictable, limited scalability.
**When to use:** Open-source, creative tools, mission-aligned products.

### 6. Affiliate / Referral Revenue
Earn commission by promoting other products.

**Advantages:** No product support burden, revenue from existing traffic.
**Disadvantages:** Dependent on partners, can feel inauthentic.
**When to use:** Content sites, review sites, comparison tools.

## Pricing Strategies

### The Value-Based Approach
Price based on the value you create, not your costs.

**Formula:**
- If your product saves 10 hours/month at $50/hour = $500/month value.
- Price at $50/month = 10x ROI for the customer.

### The Anchoring Strategy
Use pricing tiers to guide customers to your preferred plan.

**The 3-Tier Structure:**
1. **Basic ($X/month):** Just enough to get started. Captures price-sensitive users.
2. **Pro ($3X/month):** The plan you want most users to choose. Best value.
3. **Enterprise ($10X/month):** For power users and teams. Captures high-value customers.

### The Decoy Effect
Add a slightly worse option to make your target option look better.

**Example:**
- Plan A: $29/month (basic features).
- Plan B: $49/month (most features) ← Target.
- Plan C: $99/month (all features, but not much more than B).
- Plan C makes Plan B look like a steal.

## The Bootstrapper's Revenue Ladder

| Stage | MRR | Goal | Key Actions |
|-------|-----|------|-------------|
| 1 | $0-$1K | Validate payment | Launch pricing, offer LTD, manual onboarding |
| 2 | $1K-$10K | Optimize pricing | Tiered pricing, A/B test, annual discount, dunning |
| 3 | $10K-$50K | Maximize revenue | Enterprise plan, add-ons, referrals, price increases |
| 4 | $50K-$100K | Scale & diversify | New tiers, annual contracts, payment methods, NRR |

## Revenue Metrics to Track

| Metric | Formula | Target |
|--------|---------|--------|
| MRR | Monthly Recurring Revenue | Growing 10%+ monthly |
| ARR | Annual Recurring Revenue | MRR × 12 |
| LTV | Lifetime Value | ARPU / Churn Rate |
| CAC | Customer Acquisition Cost | Total Sales & Marketing / New Customers |
| LTV:CAC | LTV / CAC | > 3:1 |
| Payback Period | CAC / MRR | < 12 months |
| Churn | Lost Customers / Total Customers | < 5% monthly |
| NRR | (Starting MRR + Expansion - Churn) / Starting MRR | > 100% |

## The Art of Raising Prices

### When to Raise Prices
- You've added significant value.
- New customers are converting at high rates.
- You're underpriced compared to competitors.
- You need to fund growth or improve margins.

### How to Raise Prices
1. **Grandfather existing customers:** Keep their current price. Raise only for new customers.
2. **Give advance notice:** 30-60 days before price increase.
3. **Add value first:** Launch new features before raising prices.
4. **Offer annual lock-in:** "Lock in current price for a year by switching to annual."
5. **Segment carefully:** Raise prices for specific segments (e.g., enterprise) first.

### Handling Price Objections
- "It's too expensive." → "What would it cost you to solve this without our tool?"
- "I can build this myself." → "How many hours would that take? What's your hourly rate?"
- "The free alternative is good enough." → "What features are you missing? How much is that costing you?"

## Pricing Checklist

- [ ] Charging from day one (or at least validating willingness to pay).
- [ ] Tested at least 2 pricing models (e.g., subscription + lifetime).
- [ ] Implemented 3-tier pricing with a clear target tier.
- [ ] Tracking MRR, LTV, CAC, and churn monthly.
- [ ] LTV:CAC ratio is > 3:1.
- [ ] Payback period is < 12 months.
- [ ] Annual revenue is > 20% of total revenue (for cash flow).
- [ ] Experimented with price increases (for new customers).
- [ ] Added expansion revenue (upsells, add-ons).
- [ ] Implemented dunning to recover failed payments.
